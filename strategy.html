Business model canvas

Commercial structure at a glance

Customer segments

Primary: UK university campuses. Secondary: airports and shuttle operators. Buyer and day-to-day user can differ.

Value proposition

Safe remote monitoring, emergency intervention, lower cost than full teleoperation and scalable site-level deployment.

Channels

Direct B2B engagement, pilot deployments, demonstrations, trials and institutional procurement routes.

Customer relationships

Long-term technical partnership, deployment support, updates, monitoring and trust-building around safety assurance.

Revenue streams

One-off deployment of roughly £75k per site, annual support of roughly £60k, plus optional customisation.

Key resources

Monitoring and control software platform, engineering team, hardware stack and validation vehicle or reference setup.

Key activities

Software development, system integration, testing, validation, deployment and long-term maintenance.

Key partners and cost structure

Sensor, telecom and vehicle partners support delivery; personnel remains the dominant cost driver.

SWOT

Condensed SWOT analysis

Strengths

  • Clear niche between full autonomy and full teleoperation
  • Lower cost and faster deployment potential
  • Strong fit for controlled environments
  • Per-site model supports repeatable rollout

Weaknesses

  • Dependence on communication quality
  • Requires an underlying autonomous capability
  • Integration remains technically demanding
  • Operator performance still matters in edge cases

Opportunities

  • Growing autonomous mobility pilots in structured settings
  • Gap in mid-level supervision solutions
  • Supportive focus on safety oversight
  • Potential future extension with AI analytics and predictive monitoring

Threats

  • Advances in full autonomy may reduce perceived need
  • Teleoperation providers may move down-market
  • Regulatory shifts could slow deployment
  • Institutional caution may delay purchases

Strategic summary

The business plan concludes that the proposed system is technically and commercially plausible because it balances safety, cost and deployability. It does not attempt to replace full autonomy or transform into a full teleoperation product. Instead, it offers a selective intervention layer for environments where route structure, low speed and institutional governance make pilots possible.

The recommended path in the source document is to begin with UK campuses, validate through pilot deployment, use those results to build case studies and then extend into airports and related shuttle environments.
